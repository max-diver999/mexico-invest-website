  - question: "What is the 5-year rule in the ISR exemption?"
    answer: "The ISR exemption on primary residence sales is intended for individuals who have lived in the property, and the law has been interpreted to require the property to have served as the actual primary residence. There is also a frequency limitation: the exemption can generally be applied only once every five years per taxpayer. This prevents the exemption from being used as a repeated tax optimization tool across multiple property flips. The specific holding or use period requirements should be confirmed with a Mexican tax professional, as interpretations and SAT guidance evolve."
  - question: "How much gain can be exempted under the ISR primary residence exemption?"
    answer: "The ISR primary residence exemption has a monetary cap set in Unidades de Inversión (UDIs), Mexico's inflation-linked unit of account. The cap represents approximately MXN 700,000 to MXN 800,000 equivalent in recent years (the UDI value adjusts daily). Any gain above this threshold is subject to ISR at applicable rates. The exact cap in UDIs is confirmed annually. For gains on high-value properties, the exemption covers only a portion of the total capital gain."
  - question: "Does the ISR exemption apply to non-residents selling Mexico property?"
    answer: "Non-residents (foreigners without Mexican tax residency) generally cannot claim the primary residence ISR exemption. Non-residents selling Mexico property typically pay a withholding tax calculated either as 25% of the gross sale price or 35% of the net gain, whichever is lower, with the notario withholding the lower amount. The non-resident tax structure is separate from the resident primary residence exemption. A tax treaty between your home country and Mexico may affect your ultimate tax liability."
  - question: "What documents are required to claim the ISR exemption at closing?"
    answer: "To claim the primary residence exemption at closing, you typically present to the notario: proof of RFC (tax ID), proof that the property was your primary residence (utility bills, credencial de elector, or CURP linked to the address), and CFDI invoices for improvements made during ownership to establish cost basis. The notario calculates and applies the exemption at the time of the escritura. Missing documentation results in the notario withholding ISR at full rates."
  - question: "Can foreigners who are Mexican tax residents claim the ISR exemption?"
    answer: "Foreign nationals who qualify as Mexican tax residents, generally those who have lived in Mexico for more than 183 days in a calendar year, or who have their primary vital interests in Mexico, can potentially claim the primary residence exemption as residents, not as foreigners. Mexican tax residency is a legal status determined by SAT criteria, not solely by visa category. If you have established genuine Mexican tax residency, consult a Mexican tax attorney regarding your eligibility."
  - question: "What is the relationship between ISR and the US or Canadian tax obligations on the same sale?"
    answer: "Selling Mexico property triggers both Mexican ISR obligations and potential US or Canadian capital gains tax reporting obligations. Mexico's tax treaty with the United States (and separately with Canada) provides mechanisms to avoid double taxation, typically through foreign tax credits. ISR paid in Mexico may reduce or offset your US or Canadian tax liability on the same gain. The interaction of the two systems requires a tax professional familiar with both jurisdictions."
  - question: "What if the notario withholds ISR and I believe the exemption applies?"
    answer: "If you believe you qualified for the primary residence exemption but the notario withheld ISR, you can file for a refund through SAT after closing. This requires complete documentation of primary residence use, RFC, CFDI records, and the calculation showing the gain was within or at the exemption limit. Refund processes through SAT take months and require active follow-up. Preparing documentation before closing avoids this scenario."

Mexico's Income Tax Law (Ley del Impuesto Sobre la Renta, or Ley del ISR) treats gains on real estate sales as taxable income. At the time of the notarized closing (escrituración), the notario público calculates and withholds the applicable ISR on behalf of the seller.

The notario's ISR calculation follows a prescribed methodology:

1. **Sale price** (precio de enajenación) from the closing escritura
2. **Minus deductible cost basis** (costo comprobado de adquisición)
3. **Minus deductible improvements** (mejoras comprobadas con CFDI)
4. **Minus notario and closing cost deductions** where applicable
5. **Equals taxable gain** (ganancia)
6. **Apply applicable ISR rate** or withholding structure
7. **Minus applicable exemption** if qualifying conditions are met

The result is the ISR withheld at closing. This is a provisional withholding, sellers can file an ISR tax return to adjust the amount if the actual calculation differs from the notario's provisional figure.

## What happens if documentation is missing at closing

If you arrive at closing without complete documentation, no RFC, missing primary residence proof, or absent CFDI records for improvements, the notario will withhold ISR at standard rates without applying exemptions or deduction adjustments. This is the baseline, not a penalty. The notario is legally required to withhold unless documentation proves otherwise.

Post-closing adjustments are possible through SAT refund processes, but they are time-consuming. Preparing documentation in advance of closing is far more efficient.

| Missing document | Consequence at closing |
|---|---|
| No RFC | Notario cannot compute personalized ISR; uses generic rates |
| No primary residence proof | Primary residence exemption cannot be applied |
| No CFDI for improvements | Improvement costs not deductible from gain |
| No prior exemption history | May need sworn declaration (declaración) of five-year eligibility |

**Misconception 1: "I've owned it for five years so I'm exempt."**
The five-year rule is a frequency limit, not a holding period requirement for qualification. Owning for five years does not trigger the exemption, it prevents the exemption from being used more than once in a five-year period. Primary residence use is still required.

**Misconception 2: "As a foreigner, I don't owe Mexican tax."**
Mexico taxes gains on property located in Mexico regardless of the seller's nationality or residence status. Non-residents pay a withholding that may differ from resident rates, but the obligation exists.

**Misconception 3: "The notario will figure it out."**
The notario applies the default withholding unless you provide documentation. They are not your tax advisor and do not proactively seek exemptions on your behalf.

**Misconception 4: "Cash sales are not taxed."**
Mexico's notarios are required to report transactions and withhold ISR regardless of the payment method. The ISR obligation follows the transaction, not the payment instrument.
