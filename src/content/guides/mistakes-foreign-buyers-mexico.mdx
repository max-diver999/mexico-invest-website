    answer: "Buying ejido or informally tenured land believing it is private property, or skipping independent legal review because the seller provides a notario. Both patterns cause total or near-total capital loss more often than fideicomiso confusion ever does."
  - question: "Do foreign buyers need their own lawyer in Mexico?"
    answer: "Yes. The notario is a neutral-mandatory official, not your advocate. The seller's broker represents the seller. Independent counsel costing roughly $1,500–5,000 is standard insurance on six-figure purchases."
  - question: "Why do investors overestimate rental yields in Mexico?"
    answer: "They trust gross yield on broker decks without subtracting 20–35% management, HOA, vacancy, taxes, and fideicomiso fees. Net yields often land 200–300 basis points lower than marketing, sometimes more in oversupplied sub-markets."
  - question: "Is buying without CFDI documentation a mistake?"
    answer: "Yes for anyone planning to sell. Mexico ISR uses documented cost basis. Missing CFDI invoices at purchase and renovation inflate taxable gain on exit, a preventable six-figure error."
  - question: "Should first-time buyers use a Mexican corporation?"
    answer: "Usually no for a single coastal condo. Corporations add compliance cost without simplifying STR or ISR. Default fideicomiso unless an accountant shows clear multi-asset business rationale."
  - question: "What pre-construction mistakes are common?"
    answer: "Wiring large deposits without escrow discipline, skipping developer delivery track record review, ignoring permit status, and buying in oversupplied corridors like Tulum Region 15 at peak pricing."

    answer: "Buyers fail to read regime de condominio bylaws and discover short-term rentals banned after closing, or face special assessments that erase yield. Pretty amenities do not override written bans."
  - question: "Can I fix mistakes after closing?"
    answer: "Some issues are curable with money and time, CFDI going forward, permit applications, resale. Ejido purchases and fundamental title defects are often not fixable. Prevention at due diligence is cheaper than cure."
